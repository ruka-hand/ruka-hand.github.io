RUKA: Rethinking the Design of Humanoid Hands with Learning

Anya Zorin, Irmak Guzey, Nikhil X. Bhattasali, Lerrel Pinto,
New York University
arXiv Code API Summary Instruction

Abstract

Dexterous manipulation is a fundamental capability for robotic systems, yet progress has been limited by hardware trade-offs between precision, compactness, strength, and afford- ability. Existing control methods impose compromises on hand designs and applications. However, learning-based approaches present opportunities to rethink these trade-offs, particularly to address challenges with tendon-driven actuation and low-cost materials.

This work presents RUKA, a tendon-driven humanoid hand that is compact, affordable, and capable. Made from 3D-printed parts and off-the-shelf components, RUKA has 5 fingers with 15 underactuated degrees of freedom enabling diverse human-like grasps. Its tendon-driven actuation allows powerful grasping in a compact, human-sized form factor. To address control challenges, we learn joint-to-actuator and fingertip-to- actuator models from motion-capture data collected by the MANUS glove, leveraging the hand's morphological accuracy. Extensive evaluations demonstrate RUKA's superior reachability, durability, and strength compared to other robotic hands. Tele- operation tasks further showcase RUKA's dexterous movements. The open-source design and assembly instructions of RUKA, code, and data are available on our website.

Hardware

RUKA hand is a tendon-driven humanoid robotic hand that balances precision, compactness, strength, and cost-effectiveness. Made from 3D-printed parts and off-the-shelf components, RUKA costs under $500 and takes approximately 7 hours to assemble. It features five fingers (including an opposable thumb) with human-like dimensions and 15 degrees of freedom, 11 of which are actuated.

In performance testing, RUKA outperforms more expensive commercial alternatives - achieving 10/10 on the Kapandji reachability test and successfully executing 29 out of 33 standard GRASP taxonomy grasps. Its physical capabilities are notable, with a 6.0 kg payload capacity compared to LEAP's 4.0 kg and Allegro's 3.6 kg. RUKA demonstrates superior pinch force (2.74 N) and finger strength at both DIP/PIP joints (33.02 N) and MCP joints (16.15 N). The hand operates continuously for 20+ hours without significant performance degradation, with temperatures stabilizing during extended use.

For more instructions on hardware assembly, please visit our Technical Documentation.

Method

RUKA employs a data-driven approach to control its tendon-driven mechanism by attaching motion-capture gloves to collect fingertip positions and joint angles. The collected data trains separate neural network controllers for each finger, mapping desired fingertip positions or joint angles to motor commands with precision under 8mm in fingertip positioning.

Teleoperation

RUKA's teleoperation system integrates with OpenTeach to enable remote control through hand pose estimates from an Oculus Quest at a rate of 7Hz. The system maps human joint angles to the robot for the four main fingers while using fingertip positions for the thumb to account for morphological differences.
